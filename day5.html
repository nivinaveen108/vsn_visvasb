Nyāsam: Converting the Body to a Temple

Day 5 begins the deep dive into Nyāsam (Ritual Installation). Guruji explains that before chanting, we must make our physical bodies a fit "temple" for the Divine. We do not just recite names; we "deposit" the deity's power into our limbs.

The Sacred Installation
  • Rishi (Vyāsa): Touched on the head, as he visualized the mantra in his dhyānam.
  • Chandas (Meter): Touched on the lips, where the poetic meter vibrates.
  • Devatā (Viṣṇu): Deposited in the heart.
  • Śakti (Kṛṣṇa): Installed in the navel, the source of life-force.

By worshipping one's own feet as the Feet of the Lord during this process, the practitioner transcends their mundane identity.

The Cosmic Charioteer & Divine Weapons

Chanting requires the alignment of Body, Speech, and Mind. Kṛṣṇa is envisioned as the supreme Sārathi (Charioteer).

The Cycle of Pralaya (Rest)

The weapons of the Lord—Śaṅkha, Gadā, and Cakra—govern Creation, Sustenance, and Annihilation. Guruji provides a beautiful perspective on Pralaya (dissolution): Just as we need sleep at night to rest the body, the soul needs Pralaya as a period of cosmic rest before the next creation begins.

Dhyānam 1: Mukunda in the Milky Ocean

The first meditation śloka describes Mukunda (The Giver of Liberation) in the Kṣīrābdhi (Milky Ocean). This is not a physical beach, but a divine space where the sands are pearls and gems.

  • Attire: Garments shining like crystal and pearls.
  • Environment: White clouds forming an arch above His head, raining "Honey"—a metaphor for the sweet nectar of Mokṣa.
  • Form: Holding the Śaṅkha, Cakra, Gadā, and Nalina (Lotus).

Dhyānam 2: The Universal Anatomy (Virāt)

The second meditation focuses on the **Cosmic Form (Virāt/Trivikrama)**. Here, the practitioner meditates on the entire universe as the Lord's body.

Universal Correlation
  • Feet: The Earth (Bhūmi).
  • Navel: The Sky (Nabham).
  • Breathing: The Air (Vāyu).
  • Eyes: The Sun (to punish evil/heat) and the Moon (to protect devotees/grace).
  • Ears: The Eight Directions.
  • Abdomen: All the Oceans of the world.

Śāntākāraṃ & The Mirror Effect

Guruji analyzes the famous Śāntākāraṃ verse, highlighting Lakṣmīkāntam (the husband of Lakshmi). He explains a profound "Reciprocal Beauty" between the Lord and the Mother.

The Reflection of Eyes

The Lord: His eyes are reddish (Kamalanayana) because He is constantly looking at Mahalakshmi, who resides in a forest of red lotuses. Her beauty reflects in His eyes.

The Mother: Her eyes are blackish because she is constantly gazing at the Lord, who is the dark color of a rainy cloud (Megha-varna).

He is also Bhava-bhaya-haram—the remover of the fear of "becoming" or the future. Surrender removes the anxiety of past regrets and future worries.

Bhrigu's Test & the Śrīvatsa Mark

Why does the Lord have a mole on His chest called Śrīvatsa? Guruji recounts the story of Bhrigu Maharishi sent to test the Trinity.

  • Bhrigu kicked the Lord's chest in Vaikuṇṭha. Viṣṇu, instead of anger, massaged the Sage's foot, apologizing for His "hard chest."
  • This humility established Viṣṇu as the most Sāttvika deity.
  • However, Mahalakshmi took offense because her residence (the chest) was kicked. She left for Earth (Kolhapur), triggering the Tirupati Venkateshwara story.
  • The footprint remained as the Śrīvatsa mark—the mark of the Lord's infinite patience and love for His devotees.

Kṛṣṇa & the Pārijāta Legend

Meditation moves to Kṛṣṇa sitting under the Pārijāta Tree. Guruji explains how this heavenly tree came to Earth.

Victory over Narakāsura

Kṛṣṇa defeated Narakāsura with the help of Satyabhāmā (the Earth Goddess). To fulfill Satyabhāmā's wish, Kṛṣṇa fought Indra to bring the celestial Pārijāta tree to her garden. This symbolizes the Lord's willingness to cross any boundary to please a sincere devotee.

Kṛṣṇa's face is described as Candrānanam (Moon-like) and his eyes as Āyatam (Broad), reaching toward His ears to cast a wide glance of grace.

Mother as Mediator & Spiritual Remedies

The session concludes with the philosophy of Prapatti (Surrender). Devotees are encouraged to approach the Lord through the Divine Mother.

The Report Card Analogy

A child with bad grades goes to the mother first. She secures a promise of improvement and then convinces the strict father to sign the report card. Similarly, Mahalakshmi (Purushakāra) mediates for us, asking the Lord to overlook our sins because we are His children.

Practical Guidance:

  • Financial Debts: Chant the 65th Śloka (Lakshmi names) 10 times daily or recite the Ṛṇamocana Stotram.
  • Iconography: To see the seated "Siṃhāsana" form, visit the Kumbakonam Ramaswamy Temple.
