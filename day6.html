Foundations & Classification

Day 6 begins the transition into the core study of the names. Guruji notes that the names are categorized into four distinct types:

  • Svarūpa: Inner nature, characterized as Sat-Cit-Ānanda (Existence, Consciousness, Bliss).
  • Rūpa: Physical forms assumed across various incarnations.
  • Guṇa: Divine attributes, such as boundless love for devotees.
  • Vibhava: Divine activities or sports (Leelas) performed as Rāma or Kṛṣṇa.

Historical Commentaries

The study relies on three primary pillars of philosophy:

  • Advaita: Represented by Adi Śaṅkarācārya, who completed his commentary in just two days.
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita: Represented by Parāśara Bhaṭṭa, son of Kureśa.
  • Dvaita: Represented by Satyasandha (following the lineage of Madhvācārya).

Viśvam: The First Name & Three Causes

The first name, Viśvam, is in the neuter gender. It is derived from the root viś (to enter). It signifies that when the Lord created the universe, He entered it to provide dynamism and function.

The Potter Analogy

To create a pot, you need:

  • Upādāna (Material): The clay.
  • Nimitta (Efficient): The potter (intelligence).
  • Sahakāri (Instrumental): The wheel and tools.

Bhagavān is unique: He is all three causes. He does not look for external material; He creates it from Himself, acts as the creator, and uses His own limbs as the tools.

Viṣṇu: Entering the Creation

The name Viṣṇu also signifies entry. He is not limited by Time (Past/Present/Future), Space (India/USA/Everywhere), or Objects (the material of everything comes from Him).

The Spider Analogy

Just as a spider creates a web using material from its own saliva (acting as material, creator, and tool), Bhagavān creates the entire universe from Himself. He is compared to a universal Wi-Fi that operates billions of souls (jīvātmās) through various bodies.

Extracting Ghee: The Realization Process

If God is everywhere, why can't we see Him? Guruji provides the classic "Ghee in Milk" analogy.

The Spiritual Evolution
  • Milk: Represents the soul being "heated" by worldly struggles (Tapatraya).
  • Curing: Adding curd (Satsaṅga) to transform the milk.
  • Churning: Cultivating Sāttvika qualities through chanting.
  • Heat of Meditation: Finally extracts the Ghee (The Divine), which was always present but hidden.

Bhagavān: The Six Divine Qualities

Bhagavān is the source of all existence. He possesses the Ṣaḍguṇa (Six primary qualities):

  • Jnana: Absolute knowledge.
  • Bala: Strength.
  • Aishvarya: Lordship and control.
  • Virya: Valour.
  • Shakti: Potential/Kinetic energy.
  • Tejas: Splendor.

He provides for a being's needs even before they are born—such as mother's milk or the nutrients inside an egg. As Mahatma Gandhi noted, the Lord provides for every man's need, but not one man's greed.

Philosophical Schools & The Rope Analogy

Guruji explains how different schools view the Divine presence using the rope and the snake.

The Illusion of the Snake

In the dark, we mistake a rope for a snake and feel fear. When light appears, we realize it was always just a rope. Similarly, Advaita posits that Brahman (the rope) supports the illusion of the world (the snake). Viśiṣṭādvaita views the world as real but as the body of the Lord.

The Cell Phone Analogy: Cit, Acit, Ishvara

To understand the relationship between the soul and God, Guruji provides a modern analogy:

  • The Equipment (Acit): The physical body is the hardware—inert on its own.
  • The SIM Card (Cit): The individual soul; unique and distinct.
  • The Battery (Īśvara): The Lord's power; without it, neither the body nor the soul can function.

Q&A: Practical Insights

The session concludes with practical spiritual remedies and definitions:

  • Rama Mantra: Recommended as the best name for constant chanting.
  • Kṣetrajñaḥ: The Lord as the "knower of the field" (the body).
  • Nandaḥ: The Lord who feels joy when His devotees are happy, like a parent watching a child.
  • Puruṣaḥ: The Lord residing in the "city" of the body (which has nine holes).
